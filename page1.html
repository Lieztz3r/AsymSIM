The fluorescent lights at Sparkle's hadn't worked right in years — they'd buzz twice, flicker once, and then settle into a greenish halflight that made everything look like the bottom of an aquarium.

Nobody who worked the graveyard shift talked about what they'd seen. Not out loud. You'd notice it in the way they checked their pockets before clocking out, patting each one twice as if counting something they didn't want to name.

The mop closet at the end of aisle nine smelled like cold concrete and something else — something that moved when you weren't looking at it. Everyone knew to clean around it. Nobody ever cleaned inside it.

[ The last person who did never came back for their final paycheck. ]

The security footage from that night is kept on a drive in the manager's desk drawer. The drawer is locked with a padlock, not the standard key. Nobody remembers who put the padlock there.
